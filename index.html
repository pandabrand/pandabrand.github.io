CLASS OVERVIEW

MOVEMENTS is inspired by my restlessness. With quarantine, travel restrictions, working from home, and the virus surging, it’s hard to escape feelings of confinement. What to do about it? Find ways to move - our bodies, spirits and creative practices. My hope is that together we can investigate movement through the act of drawing. We will begin by drawing images that represent movement, focusing on figures in motion and marks on the paper that pulse and vibrate. As the course progresses, we will also use drawing to record our physical movements such as when we walk in our neighborhoods, dance in our homes, and feel our breath in our bodies. We will be expansive in our understanding of both drawing and movement.

 

 

TODAY’S CLASS

I believe that drawing is an important practice. I also believe that it can stir complex feelings and engage the ego in ways that aren’t always productive. To that end, I like to have the first class be fun and approachable. This is why I almost always start with black marker. Marker encourages drawing, rather than revising. No time is wasted on erasing. Marker inspires a devil-may-care attitude and a loose beautiful line quality.

For our first project, we will be looking to the amazing Keith Haring and to cartoons, specifically the action lines that accompany any cartoon figure in motion. Haring’s drawings were originally made for an audience on the move, drawn in the subway stations of NYC. They were public, dynamic, monumental, political, and poignant. They pulse with a vibrant rhythm and life force. Let’s strive to channel some of that joyful verve into our drawings today! Action lines are a  signature move of his. These lines not only echo movement, they can also populate and activate the space around figures. Our project today asks us to do two things: to create exaggerated images of stylized figures in motion and to invent a vocabulary of action lines, marks, and patterns.
